# Declaration of External Help and AI Assistance

In accordance with academic integrity guidelines regarding the use of external help and Large Language Models (LLMs), this appendix transparently documents the extent, nature, and boundaries of AI assistance utilized during the research and writing of this thesis. 

## 1. Transparency: List of External Help Used

Throughout the lifecycle of this project, I have utilized Large Language Models (Google's Gemini 3.1 Pro and DeepSeek v4 Pro). These tools were employed strictly as auxiliary research aides. Below is a detailed breakdown of the tools used and their specific applications:

*   **Code Generation and Debugging (Python/R):** I used LLMs to write boilerplate code, optimize data processing scripts, and debug complex syntax during the data cleaning phase. All code verified and checked by me. 
*   **Methodological Sounding Board:** I engaged with LLMs to critically challenge my empirical design. For instance, I used AI to brainstorm potential confounding variables related to "mercenary capital" and to discuss the theoretical validity of my instrumental variables and control variables.
*   **Language Refinement:** As an academic writing aid, LLMs were used to ensure grammatical correctness.
*   **Formatting and Typesetting:** AI tools were used to generate LaTeX format base on templates for tables, slides and thesis.

## 2. Original Contribution: Doing the Work and Talking About It

While AI provided valuable technical and editorial support, the intellectual contribution of this thesis remains strictly my own. I made several key achievements in this project that are independent of external assistance:

*   **Empirical Framework on DeFi Liquidity:** I conceptualized and executed a empirical framework that filled a research gap. While previous literature extensively discusses DeFi mechanisms theoretically, this study provides rigorous, causal evidence on user retention and the "mercenary capital" phenomenon.
*   **Granular On-Chain Data Engineering:** The classification of decentralized exchange (DEX) liquidity providers into precise behavioral categories—such as "stayers" and "migrators"—required deep, manual investigation into Ethereum transaction logs. Identifying the behavioral inconsistencies across different liquidity pools is a challenging task that I successfully navigated.
*   **Robustness of the Findings:** I take pride in the rigorous robustness gauntlet implemented in this thesis. By triangulating results through Survival Analysis, Multinomial Logit models, and DiD frameworks, I have ensured that the findings regarding the long-term failure of short-term liquidity incentives (Vampire Attacks) are robust and defensible. 

Compared to other studie in the field, which rely on aggregate protocol-level metrics (Total Value Locked), this thesis contributes a highly granular, user-level perspective on liquidity provision behavior. The core idea—that yield farming incentives do not purchase long-term protocol loyalty—is a conclusion born of my independent data analysis and theoretical reasoning.
